WingWatch Africa Launches in Ghana to Advance Science-Driven Conservation

Published: 2026

In 2026, WingWatch Africa was established in Accra, Ghana with a clear purpose: to strengthen conservation science in West Africa through rigorous research, collaboration, and locally grounded expertise.

While the organisation itself is new, it is built on the combined experience of four conservation scientists whose work spans ornithology, ecology, spatial analysis, and community-based conservation. Together, they bring a track record that reflects years of fieldwork, research, and applied conservation across the region.

The founding team—Joseph Kobina Daniels, Abdulai Sulemana, Joseph Kwasi Afrifa, and Samuel Etornam Tamekloe—have collectively contributed to biodiversity monitoring programs, avian ecology research, urban wildlife studies, and investigations into human–wildlife interactions.

Their work has addressed key issues such as vulture conservation, ecosystem change in human-dominated landscapes, agroforestry systems, and the role of cultural practices in shaping conservation outcomes.

Beyond field research, the team has also contributed to peer-reviewed publications and scientific discourse, reflecting a shared commitment to advancing knowledge while ensuring it remains relevant to real-world conservation challenges.

WingWatch Africa was founded on the recognition that conservation in West Africa requires more than isolated projects—it requires sustained scientific capacity, reliable data, and a deeper understanding of how ecological systems function under increasing human pressure.

The organisation aims to bridge gaps between research, policy, and practice by generating high-quality ecological data, supporting early-career scientists, and contributing to evidence-based conservation strategies.

As WingWatch Africa begins its work, it does so with a foundation rooted not just in ambition, but in experience—bringing together years of independent research into a coordinated effort to better understand and protect the region’s biodiversity.
